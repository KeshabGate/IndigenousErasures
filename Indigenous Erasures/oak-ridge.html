Oak Ridge, TN

Background on Oak Ridge

The Oak Ridge area in Tennessee, significant in the development of nuclear technology, has a deep-rooted history with the Cherokee people. This land, now home to the Oak Ridge National Laboratory, a key Manhattan Project site, was originally inhabited by various American Indian tribes, including the Cherokee, before being ceded to the U.S. Government in 1791 under the Treaty of Holston. The displacement of the Cherokee through treaties and the Indian Removal Act of 1830 culminated in the tragic Trail of Tears. Notably, individuals of Native American descent, including Cherokee members, contributed to the nuclear project at Oak Ridge. The history of this area underscores the intersection of indigenous heritage with significant scientific advancements.

Marilou Awiakta

"Where Mountains and Atom Meet"

Ancient haze lies on the mountain
smoke-blue, strange and still
a presence that eludes the mind and
moves through a deeper kind of knowing.
It is nature's breath and more -
an aura from the great I AM
that gathers to its own
spirits that have gone before.

Deep below the valley waters
eerie and hid from view
the atom splits without a sound
its only trace a fine blue glow
rising from the fissioned whole
and at its core
power that commands the will
quiet that strikes the soul
"Be still and know ... I AM."

Awiakta's poem, "When Mountains and Atoms Meet", deftly intertwines themes of nature's majesty, the profound impacts of nuclear technology, and the existential reflection on humanity's place in the world. Analyzing this poem in the context of the Anthropocene's proposed start date, marked by the "bomb spike," indigenous erasures, and the specter of nuclear apocalypse, reveals a rich tapestry of meaning and implication.

The opening stanza, "Ancient haze lies on the mountain / smoke-blue, strange and still," immediately sets a tone of timeless natural beauty and mystery. The mountains are presented as ancient entities, evoking a sense of continuity and resilience. This imagery contrasts sharply with the human-induced changes characteristic of the Anthropocene, a period marked by significant human impact on the planet's geology and ecosystems. The reference to an "ancient haze" subtly hints at the atmospheric changes resulting from nuclear testing, particularly during the mid-20th century, which is central to the "bomb spike" theory for the Anthropocene's start date.

The lines "a presence that eludes the mind and / moves through a deeper kind of knowing" suggest a spiritual or transcendent understanding of nature. This could be seen as a nod to indigenous perspectives, which often emphasize a deep, spiritual connection to the land and the natural world. In the context of indigenous erasures, these lines speak to the loss of such perspectives and wisdom due to colonial and modern industrial impacts, including nuclear development.

The poem then shifts focus with "Deep below the valley waters / eerie and hid from view / the atom splits without a sound." This stark contrast between the serene, timeless mountains and the silent, hidden splitting of the atom at the heart of nuclear technology captures the dual nature of human progress. The "fine blue glow" rising from the fissioned whole could symbolize both the awe-inspiring power of nuclear energy and the ominous threat of nuclear weapons. This duality reflects the core tension of the Anthropocene: the remarkable capabilities of human innovation coupled with its potential for destruction.

The concluding lines, "power that commands the will / quiet that strikes the soul / 'Be still and know ... I AM,'" invoke a contemplative and almost religious awe. The phrase "Be still and know ... I AM" could be interpreted as a call to recognize the profound power humans wield in the Anthropocene, akin to a god-like ability to create and destroy. It also serves as a reminder of the need for humility and reverence for the forces we have unleashed, particularly in the context of nuclear technology and the potential for apocalyptic consequences.

Overall, the poem is a poignant reflection on the contrasts and connections between the natural world and human technological advancements, particularly nuclear technology. It captures the awe, fear, and responsibility that come with living in the Anthropocene, a period defined by human impact and the looming threat of nuclear apocalypse. The poem's acknowledgment of both the beauty of the natural world and the daunting power of human invention serves as a call for deeper awareness and respect for the delicate balance that sustains life on Earth.
